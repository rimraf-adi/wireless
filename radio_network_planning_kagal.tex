  \begin{abstract}
This report walks through the radio network planning process for Kagal, a small town in Kolhapur district, Maharashtra. The goal is to design a working 4G LTE network operating on Band~3 (1800~MHz)~\cite{3gpp_36101}. We start with a ground-level site survey to understand the terrain and local building patterns, then work out a link budget that gives us $L_{\max} = 158$~dB and a maximum cell reach of about 3.68~km using the UMa NLOS path loss model from 3GPP TR~38.901~\cite{3gpp_38901}. Based on this, we lay out a \textbf{7-site macrocell grid} in a standard hexagonal ring pattern (1~centre + 6~surrounding) with a microcell overlay for high-traffic spots. The 7-cell layout ensures complete, gap-free coverage of the entire Kagal area including the NH-48 highway corridor, where macrocell overlap provides continuous coverage for fast-moving vehicles without needing a separate umbrella cell. In the second part, we simulate the fast-fading radio channel using the Clustered Delay Line (CDL) model from the same standard~\cite{3gpp_38901}, going through all twelve steps up to the final channel matrix~$H$. The Python scripts used for everything are attached at the end along with their sample outputs.

\subsection{About the Location}

Kagal sits at 16\textdegree35$'$N, 74\textdegree19$'$E, roughly 16~km south of Kolhapur city, at an elevation of about 553~m above sea level. The town has a population of around 49,000 people~\cite{census_india} spread across 8--10~km$^2$. Most buildings are 2--3 storeys tall (6--10~m), which is fairly typical of smaller towns in this part of Maharashtra.

\subsection{What Needs to Be Covered}

After looking at maps and visiting the area, we identified a few things that the network needs to handle well:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{NH-48 Highway}: This is a six-lane national highway that runs along the western edge of town. Traffic is fast-moving, so the network needs to support seamless handovers without dropped calls.
    \item \textbf{Dudhganga River}: The river creates propagation issues --- signals get absorbed and reflected off the water surface. Wherever possible, we want to avoid relying on signals to cross the river.
    \item \textbf{Main Market}: The commercial area in the town centre has the most foot traffic and the highest data demand. This is an obvious candidate for extra capacity.
    \item \textbf{Bus Stand}: Another busy spot during peak hours; travellers use voice and data services heavily here.
    \item \textbf{Residential Areas}: The older parts of town have narrow lanes and tightly packed 2--3 storey buildings. Getting good indoor signal here is a challenge.
    \item \textbf{Open Outskirts}: The agricultural land surrounding the town has very few obstructions, so signals travel much farther out here.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Working Out the Maximum Path Loss}

The simplest version of the link budget is just the difference between what we transmit and the weakest signal the phone can pick up:
\begin{equation}
L_{\max} = P_{t} - P_{r,\min} = 46 - (-104) = 150 \text{ dB}
\end{equation}

But in reality, we also gain from the antenna and lose some signal in cables and to fading. Putting it all together:
\begin{align}
L_{\max} &= P_t + G_{\text{ant}} - L_{\text{cable}} - P_{r,\min} - L_{\text{sf}} \nonumber \\
&= 46 + 18 - 2 + 104 - 8 = \boxed{158 \text{ dB}}
\end{align}

This is the number we will use for all the planning that follows.

%=============================================================================
\section{3GPP Path Loss Models}
\label{sec:pathloss_models}
%=============================================================================

All the path loss equations used here come from \textbf{3GPP TR~38.901}~\cite{3gpp_38901}, Table~7.4.1-1. Frequency is in GHz, distances in metres.

\subsection{UMa NLOS --- Our Main Model}

For UMa, the effective heights are computed by subtracting $h_E = 1$~m from both the BS and UE heights. This gives us the breakpoint distance:
\begin{equation}
d'_{\text{BP}} = \frac{2\pi\, h'_{\text{BS}}\, h'_{\text{UT}}\, f_c}{c} = 452.4 \text{ m}
\end{equation}

We also need the UMa LOS formula as a lower bound:
{\small
\begin{equation}
\text{PL}_{\text{UMa}}^{\text{LOS}} = \begin{cases}
28.0 + 22\log_{10}(d_{3D}) + 20\log_{10}(f_c) \\
\quad d_{2D} \leq d'_{BP} \\[4pt]
28.0 + 40\log_{10}(d_{3D}) + 20\log_{10}(f_c) \\
\quad - 9\log_{10}\![(d'_{BP})^2 + (h_{BS} - h_{UT})^2] \\
\quad d'_{BP} < d_{2D} \leq 5000
\end{cases}
\end{equation}}

The NLOS formula, which is the one we actually use for planning:
\begin{multline}
\text{PL}'_{\text{UMa-NLOS}} = 13.54 + 39.08\log_{10}(d_{3\text{D}}) \\
+ 20\log_{10}(f_c) - 0.6(h_{\text{UT}} - 1.5)
\end{multline}
\begin{equation}
\text{PL}_{\text{UMa}}^{\text{NLOS}} = \max\!\left(\text{PL}_{\text{UMa}}^{\text{LOS}},\; \text{PL}'_{\text{UMa-NLOS}}\right)
\end{equation}
Shadow fading standard deviation is 6.0~dB. Valid from 10~m to 5~km.

\subsection{Other Models Used}

\textbf{UMi NLOS} is used for the microcells where the antenna sits at just 10~m:
\begin{multline}
\text{PL}'_{\text{UMi-NLOS}} = 22.4 + 35.3\log_{10}(d_{3\text{D}}) \\
+ 21.3\log_{10}(f_c) - 0.3(h_{\text{UT}} - 1.5)
\end{multline}

\textbf{RMa NLOS} is used for the umbrella cell and for the open agricultural outskirts. The BS height there is 35~m. The full equation is in 3GPP TR~38.901~\cite{3gpp_38901} Table~7.4.1-1.

\subsection{Why These Models?}

\textbf{UMa fits Kagal well} because the town centre buildings are around 10--15~m (Fig.~\ref{fig:site_photos}a) and our proposed 25~m tower height matches the UMa default. The cell sizes we need (under 3~km) comfortably sit inside UMa's validity range.

\textbf{We use NLOS} as the default because, looking at the photos, most users in the residential and commercial areas will not have line-of-sight to the tower. Using NLOS gives us the worst-case path loss, which makes our design more robust.

\section{Layout Design and Cell Radius}
\label{sec:design}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{D/R Ratio and Initial Estimate}

In a hexagonal cell layout with frequency reuse factor $N$, the ratio of co-channel distance to cell radius is~\cite{rappaport2024}:
\begin{equation}
\frac{D}{R} = \sqrt{3N}
\end{equation}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{$D/R$ Ratios for Different Reuse Factors}
\label{tab:dr_ratios}
\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{%
\begin{tabular}{@{}cccc@{}}
\toprule
$N$ & $D/R$ & $D$ ($R{=}1$~km) & Interference Level \\
\midrule
1 & 1.732 & 1732~m & High (managed by ICIC) \\
3 & 3.000 & 3000~m & Moderate \\
4 & 3.464 & 3464~m & Moderate-Low \\
7 & 4.583 & 4583~m & Low \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}}%
\end{table}

Since LTE uses OFDMA with frequency reuse 1, we have $D/R = \sqrt{3} \approx 1.732$. If we start with $R \approx 1$~km, each hex cell covers about $2.598$~km$^2$. For an 8~km$^2$ town, that means 4~cells minimum. However, with smaller cells ($R = 800$~m), each hex covers $2.598 \times 0.8^2 \approx 1.66$~km$^2$, so we need at least 5--6~cells for area coverage alone. We go with \textbf{7 cells} arranged in a standard hexagonal ring (1~centre + 6~surrounding) to guarantee full, gap-free coverage with proper overlap for handovers.

\subsection{How Far Can Each Cell Reach?}

We take the UMa NLOS formula and plug in $L_{\max} = 158$~dB. Since $h_{\text{UT}} = 1.5$~m, the offset term drops out:
\begin{align}
\log_{10}(d_{3\text{D}}) &= \frac{158 - 13.54 - 5.11}{39.08} = 3.566 \nonumber \\
\boxed{R_{\max}} &= 10^{3.566} \approx \boxed{3680 \text{ m} \approx 3.68 \text{ km}}
\end{align}

Plugging 3680~m back into the formula gives $158.00$~dB, which matches $L_{\max}$ exactly.

\textbf{Why 7 cells in a hex ring?} This is the natural tessellation pattern for hexagonal cells. With 6~surrounding cells at $60^\circ$ spacing and $\sqrt{3}R$ apart from the centre, every point in the coverage area falls within at least one cell, eliminating the gaps that a 5-cell layout would leave. The path loss at every cell edge is identical (132.1~dB), leaving a comfortable margin of $158.0 - 132.1 = 25.9$~dB compared to $L_{\max}$. The total coverage comes to $7 \times 2.598 \times 0.8^2 \approx 11.6$~km$^2$, which comfortably covers the 8~km$^2$ town area with generous overlap for seamless handovers. Site~4 sits on the western bank of the Dudhganga to avoid cross-river propagation, and the NH-48 corridor between Sites~2, 3 and~6 has triple-overlap for uninterrupted highway coverage.

\section{Microcell Overlay and Highway Coverage}
\label{sec:special_cells}
%=============================================================================

On top of the seven macrocells, we deploy microcells where traffic demand is highest.

\textbf{Microcells} are small cells with 10~m antenna height and about 200--300~m radius. We put one in the \textbf{main market area} where user density spikes during business hours, and another at the \textbf{bus stand} where commuters crowd during rush hour. Running the UMi NLOS formula at 300~m:
\begin{align*}
\text{PL} &= 22.4 + 35.3\log_{10}(300) \\
& \quad + 21.3\log_{10}(1.8) \\
&= 22.4 + 87.44 + 5.44 = 115.3 \text{ dB} \ll L_{\max}\;\checkmark
\end{align*}

\textbf{Highway coverage without an umbrella cell}: With the 7-cell ring, the NH-48 corridor is covered by the overlap of Sites~2, 3, and~6. Site~6 (West / Pimpalgaon Khurd) sits on the western side of the highway while Sites~2 and~3 bracket it from the north and south. This triple overlap means a vehicle at highway speed will always be within range of at least one, and often two, macrocells during any handover transition. Adding a separate umbrella cell on top of this would be redundant --- the hexagonal tessellation already provides continuous highway coverage.

\section{Part 2: Simulating the Radio Channel}
\label{sec:part2}
%=============================================================================

In this section we simulate what the radio channel actually looks like at small time scales, following the step-by-step procedure in 3GPP TR~38.901~\cite{3gpp_38901}, Section~7.5. We use the CDL channel model with UMa NLOS parameters.

\textbf{Steps 1--4} set up the large-scale picture:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Antenna setup}: We model a $2 \times 8$ MIMO link --- the base station has 4~columns of cross-polarised elements (8~total, spaced at $\lambda/2$), and the user has 2~elements. The user is placed 583~m from Site~1.
    \item \textbf{Propagation type}: NLOS, same as the planning phase.
    \item \textbf{Path loss}: The UMa NLOS model gives 126.7~dB at this distance. Shadow fading is drawn randomly from a normal distribution with 6~dB standard deviation.
    \item \textbf{Large scale parameters}: Delay spread, angular spreads (ASD, ASA, ZSD, ZSA) are generated using correlated log-normal distributions. We use Cholesky decomposition on the cross-correlation matrix from Table~7.5-6~\cite{3gpp_38901}.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Steps 5--9} generate the multipath structure:
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
    \item \textbf{Cluster delays}: 20~clusters are drawn from an exponential distribution with $r_\tau = 2.3$.
    \item \textbf{Cluster powers}: Each cluster gets a power from an exponential PDP with per-cluster shadowing of 3~dB. Weak clusters (below $-25$~dB from the strongest) are dropped.
    \item \textbf{Angles}: Azimuth angles (AOA and AOD) follow a wrapped Gaussian distribution, and zenith angles (ZOA and ZOD) follow a Laplacian. The ray offsets from Table~7.5-3 are applied with the right cluster spreads.
    \item \textbf{Ray coupling}: Rays within each cluster are randomly shuffled to pair up AOD with AOA, and ZOD with ZOA.
    \item \textbf{Cross-polarisation}: Each ray gets an XPR value drawn from $\mathcal{N}(7, 3^2)$~dB.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Steps 10--12} assemble the final channel:
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
    \item \textbf{Random phases}: Four phase values (one for each polarisation combination) are drawn uniformly from $[-\pi, \pi]$ for every ray.
    \item \textbf{Channel matrix}: The full $H_{u,s,n}^{\text{NLOS}}(t)$ is built by summing over all rays and clusters, including antenna patterns, array steering vectors, the polarisation coupling matrix, and Doppler shift at 3~km/h walking speed.
    \item \textbf{Applying path loss}: The large-scale path loss and shadow fading from Step~3 are applied to get the final channel coefficients.
\end{enumerate}
